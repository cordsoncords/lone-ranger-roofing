A Burnet homeowner notices a leak after a hailstorm rolls through the Hill Country and assumes the storm caused it. Sometimes that's exactly right. Sometimes the storm finished off a roof that was already most of the way to failing on its own, and the hail gets blamed for damage that was years in the making. Telling the two apart matters more than most homeowners realize, because it decides whether insurance pays for the fix.

We've inspected enough roofs in Burnet after enough storms to know that the sun out here is as hard on a roof as any hailstorm. Long, hot summers bake shingles for years before a storm ever shows up, and that baseline condition changes what a storm actually did versus what was already wearing thin.

## The Signs That Point to Storm Damage

Storm damage tends to leave a pattern that matches the event. Hail bruises show up as dark, circular spots where granules have been knocked loose, often clustered on the side of the roof that faced the wind. Wind damage shows up as shingles that are lifted, creased, or missing outright, usually along ridgelines and edges where wind pressure hits hardest. Both of these point to a specific event on a specific date, which is exactly what an insurance claim needs to establish.

Fresh damage also tends to expose a different color underneath. A cracked or lifted shingle from a recent storm reveals granules and a mat that haven't faded the way the surrounding roof has, since sun exposure fades shingles evenly over time. That contrast is one of the clearest signs a [roof inspection](/residential-roofing/roof-inspection) can point to when documenting a Burnet storm claim.

## The Signs That Point to Age

Age-related failure looks different. Shingles curling up at the edges across wide sections of the roof, granules washing out evenly rather than in a hail pattern, and brittle shingles that crack when touched rather than from impact all point to a roof nearing the end of its service life. This kind of wear shows up gradually and evenly, not in the localized clusters a storm leaves behind.

A roof's age matters as much as its appearance. Most asphalt shingle roofs in Central Texas are built to last two to three decades, and a 22-year-old roof showing granule loss after a mild storm is telling a different story than a five-year-old roof with the same symptom. We factor in the roof's condition before the storm ever happened, not the way it looks after.

## Why This Distinction Decides What Gets Covered

Insurance covers sudden, storm-caused damage. It doesn't cover a roof that failed because it reached the end of its natural lifespan, and adjusters look closely for evidence of which one they're dealing with. A claim built on an honest read of the roof, showing exactly where storm damage occurred and where age-related wear was already present, holds up far better than one that tries to pass off every flaw as storm-related.

This works in the homeowner's favor too. An inspection that draws a clear line between the two gives an adjuster less to argue about, which tends to move the claim faster and produce a fairer number. It also protects a Burnet homeowner from a [roof replacement](/residential-roofing/roof-replacement) pitch that's really about age dressed up as storm damage, since some crews lean on hail season to sell replacements the roof would have needed anyway in a year or two, storm or not.

## What to Do If You're Not Sure

The honest answer is that most homeowners can't tell the difference from the ground, and that's fine. What matters is getting someone on the roof who documents both the storm-related damage and the roof's overall condition, then explains plainly which is which. That's the whole point of an inspection built for a claim rather than a quick sales pitch.

## FAQ

**Can hail damage an old roof and a new roof the same way?**
No. An older roof with brittle shingles is more likely to crack under hail impact than a newer roof with more flexible material, even from the same storm.

**Will my insurance deny a claim if my roof is old?**
Not automatically. Insurers cover storm damage regardless of roof age, but they'll scrutinize older roofs more closely to separate storm impact from existing wear.

**How can I tell if my roof damage is from a recent storm?**
Fresh damage usually looks different in color and texture from the surrounding shingles, since sun-faded shingles won't match the exposed material underneath a new crack or granule loss.

**Should I get an inspection even if I don't see obvious damage?**
Yes. Storm damage isn't always visible from the ground, and a roof that looks fine can still have granule loss or cracked shingles that show up clearly on a closer inspection.
